# 16 · Negotiation & Leveling

> You did the hard part — got the offer. This stage is high-leverage and most engineers under-play it.
> A calm, informed negotiation can move total comp meaningfully with **zero** downside if done
> respectfully. This file covers **leveling** (getting placed at the right rung) and **negotiation**
> (the package), with the equity mechanics that trip people up at private labs.

> Disclaimer: structures and numbers vary by company, location, and year, and shift constantly. Treat
> everything here as **framework, not quotes** — verify current bands on levels.fyi / Glassdoor / your
> network. Nothing here is legal or financial advice.

---

## Leveling: get this right first

**Level drives everything** — comp band, scope, and your trajectory. Negotiating $10k of base matters
far less than landing one level higher. Frontier labs use IC ladders roughly like:

| Rough rung | Typical signal | Industry-ish label |
|------------|----------------|--------------------|
| Entry | Executes well-scoped tasks | SWE I / L3 |
| Mid | Owns features independently | SWE II / L4 |
| **Senior** | Owns ambiguous projects, sets technical direction, mentors | Senior / L5 |
| Staff | Drives cross-team strategy, deep technical leadership | Staff / L6 |
| Principal+ | Org-/field-level impact | Principal / L7+ |

How to influence your level:
- **Target the level your scope matches**, not your years. "Senior" is about *ambiguity owned + impact +
  influence*, not tenure. Bring evidence: projects you scoped and drove, not tasks you completed.
- If you suspect a **down-level**, ask directly: *"What would it take to be considered at the next
  level?"* Sometimes an extra panel or a scope conversation fixes it.
- A higher level usually beats a higher number at the same level — it compounds in refreshers, future
  raises, and the next job's baseline.
- **Down-leveling risk** is real after a strong loop; counter it with concrete scope/impact stories
  ([06-behavioral-mission](06-behavioral-mission.md), [13-take-home-portfolio](13-take-home-portfolio.md)).

## The compensation package (know every component)

Total comp is **not** the base salary. Negotiate the whole thing:

- **Base salary** — cash; most stable, smallest lever at senior+ (bands are tighter).
- **Equity** — usually the **largest** component at frontier labs and the biggest lever. See mechanics below.
- **Signing bonus** — one-time cash; the **easiest** thing to get added or increased; often used to
  bridge a band or offset forfeited comp from your current employer.
- **Annual/target bonus** — % of base, performance-linked (varies by company).
- **Equity refreshers** — additional grants over time; ask about the *typical* refresh so you don't hit
  a "**cliff**" in year 4 when the initial grant fully vests.
- **Benefits & perks** — 401(k) match, health, learning budget, relocation, remote flexibility.

> Compare offers on a **4-year total-comp** basis (initial grant ÷ 4 + base + bonus), and ask about
> refresh expectations — a big year-1 grant with no refreshers can be worse than a steady one.

## Equity at a private lab (the part people get wrong)

Frontier labs are often **private**, so equity isn't liquid stock. Understand:

- **RSUs vs options.** RSUs = shares you receive on vesting (value = share price). Options = the *right
  to buy* at a fixed **strike price**; worth `current − strike`. Most large labs grant RSUs.
- **Vesting & cliff.** Classic: 4-year vest, **1-year cliff** (nothing vests until 12 months, then
  monthly/quarterly). Some front-/back-load; read the schedule.
- **Double-trigger RSUs.** At private companies, RSUs often vest only when **both** (a) the time vests
  *and* (b) a liquidity event (IPO/tender) occurs — so "vested" ≠ "sellable" until liquidity.
- **409A valuation** — the company's official private share price; your grant's paper value uses it. It
  can rise (upside) or be marked down.
- **Liquidity** — how do you actually get cash? Ask about **tender offers** / secondary sales and their
  history. No liquidity path = paper wealth.
- **Strike price (options)** — lower is better; ask the current 409A and your strike.
- **Tax** — RSUs taxed as income at vest; options have AMT/strike complexity. Get a professional for
  large grants.

Questions to ask about equity: *"RSUs or options? What's the current 409A / share price used? Vesting
schedule and cliff? Single- or double-trigger? What's the liquidity history (tenders)? What's a typical
refresher at my level?"*

## Negotiation: principles (ethical, effective)

1. **Be genuinely enthusiastic, then negotiate.** "I'm excited to join — I'd like to talk about the
   package" is the whole tone. Negotiation is expected and professional; it won't rescind a real offer.
2. **Don't anchor yourself low.** When asked for current comp or expectations early, deflect: *"I'm
   focused on finding the right role and level; I'm confident we can align on a fair package once
   there's an offer."* Let them make the first number where you can.
3. **Negotiate from data, not feelings.** Cite the band (levels.fyi / peers), your competing offers,
   and your scope. "Based on senior bands and a competing offer, I was hoping for X" beats "I want more."
4. **A competing offer is the strongest lever** — real ones only. If you have one, share the *structure*
   and your genuine preference: *"I'd rather be here; can we close the gap?"*
5. **Negotiate the whole package.** If base is capped by band, pivot to **sign-on, equity, level, start
   date, or relocation.** There's almost always a flexible lever.
6. **Ask, then go quiet.** State your ask clearly and stop talking. Silence is not your job to fill.
7. **Get it in writing.** Confirm every number in the written offer before you resign anywhere. Verbal
   ≠ binding.
8. **Never bluff a competing offer.** Lying is unethical and easily backfires — and these communities
   are small.

## What's negotiable (typical)

| Usually flexible | Sometimes | Rarely |
|------------------|-----------|--------|
| Sign-on bonus | Base (within band) | The leveling ladder itself |
| Equity grant size | Level (with justification) | Benefits/401(k) structure |
| Start date | Relocation / remote | Vesting schedule |

## A clean script (adapt it)

> "Thank you — I'm really excited about the team and the mission. I've looked at senior bands and I have
> another offer in play, though this is my first choice. To make the decision easy, could we improve the
> equity and add a signing bonus? If we can get to roughly **[X total / Y sign-on]**, I'm ready to sign."

Then stop. Let them respond. Repeat once if needed; don't nickel-and-dime endlessly — decide your target
and your walk-away in advance.

## Mission-driven labs: still negotiate

At Anthropic/OpenAI-type places, candidates sometimes feel they "shouldn't" negotiate because of the
mission. **Wrong.** Being paid fairly and being mission-aligned aren't in tension; good orgs respect a
professional negotiation. Lead with genuine enthusiasm for the work, negotiate respectfully, and don't
let mission framing talk you out of fair comp.

## Red flags & due-diligence questions

- **Exploding offers** (sign in 24–48h) or heavy pressure → a yellow/red flag; ask for reasonable time.
- **Hand-wavy equity** ("it'll be worth a fortune") with no 409A, schedule, or liquidity answer.
- **Vague level/scope** — get the title, level, and manager in writing.
- Ask: runway/funding, team you'd join, on-call expectations, promotion cadence, refresher policy, and
  what success looks like in 6–12 months.

## Decision framework (beyond the number)

Weigh: **growth & scope**, **team & manager**, **mission fit**, **trajectory** (what doors it opens),
and **comp** — in that order for most careers. The best-paying offer isn't always the best offer; a
steeper learning curve at a great team usually compounds faster than a marginal comp delta.

> Bottom line: **land the right level**, evaluate **total 4-year comp** (not base), understand the
> **equity mechanics and liquidity**, and negotiate **respectfully from data** with a real alternative.
> A 20-minute conversation here can outweigh months of salary — do it calmly, honestly, and in writing.
