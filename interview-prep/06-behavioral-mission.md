# 06 · Behavioral & Mission Fit

> Frontier labs weight values and collaboration heavily — a brilliant engineer who can't communicate,
> collaborate, or reason about impact is a no-hire. This round tests **judgment, ownership, humility,

## Why Anthropic / mission fit

**Q: Why Anthropic? / Why do you want to work here?**
Be specific and honest. A strong answer has three parts: (1) **the mission** — you want to work on
making powerful AI **safe and beneficial**, not just capable; (2) **specific evidence** you've engaged
— Constitutional AI, the Responsible Scaling Policy, interpretability work, Claude's behavior, their
research culture; (3) **fit** — how your skills (serving, evals, agents, training) map to their needs,
and why *this* environment (safety-first, research-driven) suits how you want to work.

> Avoid: generic "you're the best lab" flattery, money/prestige framing, or anything suggesting you
> only care about capability/AGI race dynamics with no safety thought.

**Q: How do you think about AI safety personally?**
Show a **balanced, non-doomer, non-dismissive** view: powerful AI is enormously beneficial *and*
carries real risks (misuse + misalignment) that scale with capability; the responsible path is to
build the measurement, oversight, and guardrails alongside capabilities. Tie it to concrete engineering
you'd do (evals, red-teaming, defense-in-depth). Sincerity > rehearsed talking points.

**Q: Where do you want to grow / what do you want to work on?**
Connect your interests to their actual work (e.g. agent reliability + evals, inference efficiency,
alignment evaluation). Show you've thought about contributing, not just learning.

## The STAR framework

Structure every behavioral answer as **STAR** — it keeps you concrete and concise:

- **S**ituation — context in 1–2 sentences (what, when, your role).
- **T**ask — the goal/challenge and *your* responsibility.
- **A**ction — what **you** specifically did (use "I", show decisions + trade-offs).
- **R**esult — the outcome, **quantified**, plus what you learned.

Keep it ~2–3 minutes. Lead with the headline, then detail. Emphasize **your** decisions and reasoning,
not just the team's outcome.

## Build your story bank

Prepare **6–8 reusable stories** from real experience; most questions are remixes of these. Aim to
cover:

1. **Shipped impact** — a project you drove end-to-end with measurable results.
2. **Hard technical problem** — deep debugging / a non-obvious solution.
3. **Failure / mistake** — what broke, your role in it, what you changed afterward.
4. **Conflict / disagreement** — with a peer or manager; how you resolved it constructively.
5. **Ambiguity** — a vague problem you scoped and made progress on.
6. **Prioritization / trade-off** — saying no, cutting scope, or choosing under constraints.
7. **Influence without authority** — getting others aligned on a direction.
8. **Learning fast** — ramped on something new under pressure.

For each, write S/T/A/R bullets and a **quantified** result. Have a **safety/quality-over-speed** story
ready — it resonates strongly here.

## Common questions & how to answer

**Q: Tell me about a project you're proud of.**
Pick one with real ownership and measurable impact. Cover the problem, *your* technical decisions and
trade-offs, how you measured success, and what you'd do differently. Depth on **your** contribution.

**Q: Tell me about a time you failed.**
Pick a **real** failure with genuine stakes (not a humblebrag). Own your part honestly, focus on the
**lesson** and the concrete change you made afterward, and ideally how it improved a later outcome.
Self-awareness and growth are the signal.

**Q: A time you disagreed with someone.**
Show you can **disagree respectfully and commit**: you sought to understand their view, brought data,
proposed an experiment to resolve it, and either changed your mind on evidence or aligned on a path.
Avoid "I was right and they were wrong" framing.

**Q: A time you handled an ambiguous/underspecified problem.**
Show how you **decomposed** it, made assumptions explicit, found the smallest informative experiment,
and iterated. Comfort with ambiguity is core to research-adjacent engineering.

**Q: How do you handle competing priorities / tight deadlines?**
Demonstrate explicit prioritization (impact vs effort), communicating trade-offs to stakeholders,
cutting scope deliberately, and **not** silently dropping quality/safety. Show you negotiate scope
rather than just working more hours.

**Q: A time you took initiative / went beyond your role.**
Ownership signal: you saw a gap (a missing eval, a reliability issue, tech debt), took responsibility,
and drove a fix without being asked.

**Q: How do you give/receive critical feedback?**
Direct, kind, specific, timely; you treat feedback on your work as data and update. Bring an example
where feedback changed your approach.

## Values they screen for

Frontier labs (Anthropic especially) tend to reward:
- **Mission-first** — safety and benefit over pure capability/speed.
- **Honesty & calibration** — say "I don't know," quantify uncertainty, don't oversell.
- **High trust / low ego** — collaborative, share credit, disagree-and-commit.
- **Empiricism** — act on evidence; run the experiment; measure before claiming.
- **Pragmatism** — "do the simple thing that works," then iterate.
- **Ownership** — end-to-end responsibility, including the unglamorous parts (evals, monitoring).
- **Care for users & society** — think about real-world impact and second-order effects.

Demonstrate these *through stories*, don't just assert them.

## Questions to ask them

Asking thoughtful questions is part of the evaluation. Good ones:
- How does the team balance shipping speed against safety/eval rigor in practice?
- How do research and engineering collaborate day-to-day here?
- What does success look like for this role in the first 6–12 months?
- How are decisions made when safety considerations conflict with a product deadline?
- What's the hardest unsolved problem the team is working on right now?
- How does the team think about evals — what's mature, what's still hard?

Avoid asking only about perks/logistics in technical rounds.

---

## Red flags to avoid
- Bad-mouthing past employers/teammates.
- Taking sole credit for team work (or vague "we" with no personal contribution).
- No questions, or only comp/logistics questions in a technical round.
- Overclaiming / not admitting knowledge gaps (calibration matters more than bluffing).
- Dismissing safety as hype — or doom-posting with no constructive engineering view.
- Rambling. Use STAR; respect the time box; let them steer.

---

### How to perform in this round
Be **specific, honest, and concise**. Quantify results. Center **your** decisions and reasoning. Show
genuine, evidence-based mission alignment. Calibrated humility ("here's what I'd do differently") reads
as senior; bravado reads as junior.
