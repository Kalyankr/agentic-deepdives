## Interview answers

### Q: "Why do you want to work here / in AI?"

This is asked *everywhere* and screens harder than people think. Mission-driven labs (Anthropic, DeepMind, OpenAI) use it to filter for genuine, specific motivation over hype.

**The structure of a strong answer — three beats:**

1. **A specific technical hook** — something you actually did, recently, that shows you engage with the field unprompted.
2. **Why *this* problem** — connect that experience to the team's actual work, showing you understand what they do.
3. **Why *this* lab** — a genuine reason tied to their mission/approach, not flattery.

**Model answer (alignment role at Anthropic):**

> "Last month I reproduced the DPO paper on a 1B model and was surprised how much more stable it was than my PPO baseline — no reward model to babysit, no reward hacking, and I wrote up *why* the implicit-reward derivation makes that happen. That pulled me toward post-training, because it's where capability and safety actually meet: the same techniques that make a model helpful are the ones that make it honest. I want to work here specifically because Constitutional AI treats values as something explicit and auditable rather than implicit in a reward model — that's the version of the problem I find myself thinking about on weekends."

**Why this works:** It opens with **evidence** (reproduced a paper, has an opinion, wrote it up), shows **technical depth** (knows *why* DPO is stable), and lands on a **specific, genuine** reason for the lab (Constitutional AI), not "AI is the future."

**The weak version to avoid:**

> "AI is the future and I'm really passionate about it. Your company is doing amazing work and I'd love to be part of it."

This is indistinguishable from every other applicant. It has no evidence, no specifics, and no signal that you'd be good at the job.

**The deeper point (the chapter's thesis):** Everything in this book exists to give you the *evidence* that makes the strong answer possible. You can't fake the first beat — you have to have actually built, reproduced, and written things. Motivation questions aren't separate from technical prep; they're where your technical proof-of-work pays off.

---

### Bonus: turning this into a repeatable framework

Keep a running list of **3–5 "hooks"** — recent things you built or reproduced that you can speak about for five minutes each, defending every decision. Before any interview, map each hook to:

- the **concept** it demonstrates (e.g., "this shows I understand KL as a leash"),
- the **team** it's relevant to,
- the **next step** you'd take if you kept going (shows you think beyond the demo).

That list is also your answer to "walk me through a project" (Chapter 19's self-check #6) and "what are you most proud of." One artifact, many questions.
